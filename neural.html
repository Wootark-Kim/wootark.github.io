Basics of Neural Network Structure

A neural network structure as 3 elements:


  1. input
  2. weights
  3. bias

The input is the value that comes from other nodes (aka "neurons"). The weights can be thought up as the "preference" strength of that data value. The higher the value, the more "important" that value is compared to other inputs. The bias is the node's likelihood to send a signal.

As you can see, the resemblance its quite strong, hence the name "neural network" as it took inspiration from the neuron architecture found in the physical world.

To further expand the analogy, the input is simply the electrical signal that originates from another neuron. The weight is probably how "strong" and well connected the dendrites are (since it is receiving signals) in relation to that other neuron it is connected to. The bias is the node's "resting potential."

A human neuron has a general resting potential of -70 mV. For a node, the value of the bias determines whether it will likely continue to send the signal it received to another node. Having a low or negative bias value means it may not pass along the signal it just received to another node and will require a greater strength value of the received data to overcome this hindrance. Thus the lower the bias value, the greater the threshold of the node's activation. The higher the bias value, the lower the threshold of the node's activation.
